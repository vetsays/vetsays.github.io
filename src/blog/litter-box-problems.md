When a cat stops using the litter box, it's communicating — not misbehaving. Work through these steps in order.

## Step 1: Rule out medical causes first

Urinary infections, crystals, and other conditions make the box hurt, so cats avoid it. **Any sudden change in litter habits deserves a vet check before anything else** — especially in male cats, where blockages can be life-threatening.

## Step 2: Audit the box setup

- **Number:** one box per cat, plus one extra.
- **Location:** quiet, easy to reach, never next to noisy appliances.
- **Cleanliness:** scoop daily; cats are fastidious.
- **Style:** many cats dislike covered or self-cleaning boxes — try an open, roomy one.

## Step 3: Reconsider the litter

Most cats prefer **unscented, fine-grained clumping litter**. If you recently switched brands, that change alone can be the culprit.

## Step 4: Address stress

New pets, visitors, or a schedule change can all trigger avoidance. Predictable routines and vertical space help a cat feel secure.

## A note on cleanup

Clean accidents with an **enzymatic cleaner**, not ammonia-based products — leftover scent invites repeat visits to the same spot. Solve the *why*, and the behavior usually follows.

The physical exam is the one skill you'll use every single day in practice. The trick isn't memorizing more — it's doing the same thorough sequence _every time_ so nothing gets missed. Here's the framework I wish I'd locked in earlier.

## Start before you touch the patient

Observation is half the exam. From across the room, note **mentation, posture, breathing pattern, and gait**. A patient that "looks wrong" often is — trust that first impression and let the hands-on exam explain it.

## Take a consistent head-to-tail route

Pick an order and never break it:

1. **Head:** eyes, ears, oral cavity, mucous membrane colour and capillary refill time.
2. **Neck & chest:** lymph nodes, then auscultate heart and lungs in a quiet room.
3. **Abdomen:** systematic palpation, quadrant by quadrant.
4. **Limbs & skin:** joints, paws, coat, any lumps.
5. **Tail end:** perineum, and don't skip the rectal when it's indicated.

## A memory aid for vitals

For every patient, anchor on **TPR** — Temperature, Pulse, Respiration — plus weight and body condition score. These numbers turn "seems off" into something you can track over time.

## Write it down the same way every time

A consistent record makes patterns jump out on the recheck — and protects you. SOAP (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan) exists for a reason.

## The real secret

Repetition - Examine every healthy animal you can so the _abnormal_ announces itself. Normal has to live in your hands before you can recognize the exceptions.
